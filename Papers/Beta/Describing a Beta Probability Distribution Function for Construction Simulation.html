    Abstract

    A typical proposed application for a construction simulation model is to assess the productivity of a future operation. This type of application inherently presents the modeler with a situation where there is only a limited amount of data available for choosing an underlying probability distribution function (PDF). This paper presents a formulation for developing a Beta PDF for use in construction simulation modeling. The hypothesis of this paper is that there is a ratio that relates the 75th percentile to the mode of the activity duration. The research demonstrates that using such ratios, along with the minimum, mode, and maximum activity durations, results in estimates of a beta PDF that accurately describes the underlying duration distribution of construction activities. In the methodology proposed here the minimum and maximum activity durations are predicted using deterministic methods based on the physical characteristics of the job and equipment employed. The beta-shaped parameters a and b are estimated using a procedure for fitting beta distributions to activity times when sample data are not available.

This type of application inherently presents the mod-eler with a situation where there is only a limited amount of dataavailable for choosing an appropriate probability distributionfunction(PDF)to use in the simulation. In the absence of data,when attempting to utilize simulation models, an expert will usesubjective estimates about a given activity duration to derive aPDF to model the distribution of the process under study.While simulation modeling has proven beneficial in innumer-able applications across many industries, it has failed to win wideacceptance by construction practitioners. One of the reasons forthis lack of acceptance is the question of the underlying PDF.Professionals in the industry understand that the PDF, which canbe considered as the heart of a simulation model, is an unknowndimension. The practicing construction professional has very littleconfidence in the subjective selection of a PDF for modeling anactivity and this lack of confidence is primarily due to a commonsense appreciation of the unpredictable nature of constructionprocesses.The hypothesis of this paper is that there is a ratio that relatesthe 75th percentile duration of an activity to the mode of theactivity. This study specifically investigates the ratios correspond-ing to the load, haul, dump, and return operations of off-highwaytrucks used in construction.Finding a ratio between various activity–time characteristics toestimate the inputs of a simulation model is not a new approach,for example, scheduling personnel at Kennedy Space Center todetermine the mode value for ground activity duration of spaceshuttle operations apply a common multiplier to the estimatedminimum(Wilson et al. 1982). The research demonstrates thatusing such ratios along with the minimum, mode, and maximumactivity durations results in estimates of a beta PDF that accu-rately describes the underlying duration distribution of such con-struction activities. It is expected, based on a limited test case,that the haul cycle productivity results from simulation modelsusing the estimated beta PDFs will not vary significantly fromthose found by using actual project cycle data.Fig. 1 shows a representation of the procedure followed in theresearch to compare the productivity results obtained through thesubjective estimation of the activities duration to the productivityresults predicted when actual project data is used.Defining a Probability Density Functionfor Construction OperationsEstimation of the parameters of the PDF is controlled by dataavailability. When data are available, the expert can use a numberof numerical methods to compute the parameters of the underly-ing PDF. In the absence of data, an expert must use subjectiveinformation to estimate the parameters of the PDF.It is often recommended that, in order to model the durationparameter of a construction activity in an efficient and accurateway, a flexible family of PDFs capable of attaining a wide varietyof shapes should be used. izk and Halpin(1992)demonstrated through a modifiedb1–b2plane analysis that most earthmoving construction operations canbe described by the beta PDF.According to MacCrimmon and Rayvec(1964)and Swansonand Pazer(1971), the distribution used to model the duration of awork task should:1.Be continuous over the entire range,2.Have a unique mode in the range, and3.Have two positive abscissa intercepts(i.e., the distributionshould be bound between two positive limits).The beta PDF satisfies all of these conditions, it is a continu-ous distribution defined over a range, both of its end points arefixed at exact locations, and it belongs to the flexible family ofdistributions.The shape of the beta PDF depends on the choice of its pa-rametersaandb. The parameters are any non-negative real num-ber and, depending on their values, the beta PDF generated willhave the “U,” the “J,” the triangle or the general bell shape of theunimodal function. The ability to specify the parameters is con-trolled by data availability. When actual data are available, theexpert can use the method of moments(MM), the maximum like-lihood(MLE), the ordinary least squares(OLS), or other numeri-cal methods to arrive at estimates for the parameters of the un-derlying distribution. The benefit of using one method over theother has been the subject of many research papers in statistics aswell as engineering. Early researchers tended to use the MM be-cause of calculation ease. Today, with computers, the more accu-rate MLE and OLS methods have come into greater use.A unique beta PDF is defined by four parameters. These arethe minimum and maximum activity duration, as well as the betaPDF-shaped parametersaandb.In the methodology proposed here, the minimum and maxi-mum activity durations are predicted using deterministic methodsbased on the physical characteristics of the job and equipmentemployed. The beta-shaped parametersaandbare estimated us-ing a procedure for fitting beta distributions to activity timeswhen sample data are not available. This procedure is imple-mented using a public domain microcomputer-based softwaresystem calledVIBES(AbouRizk et al. 1991).To determine the shape parameters of a unique beta PDF,VIBESuses a combination of four activity–duration characteris-tics, two of which must be the maximum and minimum duration.The possible characteristic combinations are the maximum andminimum activity durations and either:(1)The mean and stan-dard deviation,(2)the mean and a selected percentile,(3)themode and a selected percentile, or(4)two selected percentiles.The difficulties in eliciting an accurate subjective estimation ofthe statistical characteristics such as mode, mean, variance, orselected percentiles have been the subject of a variety of researchprojects across the fields of experimental psychology, electricalengineering, and mathematics as described in the next section.Experiments in psychology—summarized by Peterson andMiller(1964)and Peterson and Beach(1967)—have indicatedthat in the case of estimating the mean of a skewed distribution,there is a tendency to bias the estimate of the mean toward themedian. Alpert and Raiffa in their 1969 progress report on thetraining of probability assessors, recommended the direct fractileassessments as the most reliable method and less prone to bias.This led Apostolakis and Mosleh(1982)to recommend that “as-sessors use percentiles to quantify their beliefs and to avoid thedirect assessment of other measures like mean value or standarddeviation.”Alpert and Raiffa(1969)and Lichtenstein et al.(1977),through experimental psychology, indicated that subjective esti-mates for certain percentiles of a population can be reasonablyaccurate, especially for the 25th, 50th, or 75th percentiles; theseare also known as the lower, median, and upper quartiles. Fromtheir research, the results of an estimate using the quartile ap-proach were more accurate than those obtained through the tertilemethod, i.e., the fractiles 33rd and 67th, and the lower and upperfractiles procedure, i.e., the fractiles 10th and 90th.The beta distribution is a continuous PDF bounded over arange; that is, the endpoints, or minimum and maximum dura-tions. According to Wilson et al.(1982), an expert can estimatethe endpoints of an activity duration distribution somewhat easilyand accurately due to the expert’s familiarity with the technologi-cal constraints on the target activity. For construction applica-tions, the mode or duration occurring most frequently, of a con-struction activity duration, can be elicited accurately from anexpert using deterministic methods. The endpoints and a value forthe mode define a number of different beta PDFs. To fit a uniquebeta distribution inVIBES, a percentile of the PDF must also bespecified.In this study, the percentile employed to specify a unique betadistribution is the 75th percentile as suggested by the investiga-tions of Wilson et al.(1982)and Lichtenstein et al.(1977). Inputcombinations using the mean and/or the standard deviations wererejected on the basis of the study presented by Peterson andMiller(1964)which explains the difficulty of assessing the meanand standard deviation of skewed distributions.Estimating Probability Density FunctionCharacteristicsThe minimum duration for each activity is calculated determinis-tically using the equipment manufacturer’s performance data(Caterpillar Performance Handbook 2002)and a set of physicalsite constraints. In this study, the most likely duration, or mode, ispredicted by dividing the activity minimum duration by a jobefficiency factor of 0.80. It was assumed that a typical job effi-ciency for a day shift is a 50 min / h, or an efficiency factor of0.83 and the efficiency of a night shift is approximated to be a45 min / h, or a factor of 0.75(Schaufelberger 1999; Nunnally2000; Peurifoy and Schexnayder 2002). Analyzing a total of 54,300 haulcycles from an actual heavy construction project provided thebasis for these factors. The haul cycles analyzed were collectedon the Eastside Reservoir Project in California by a fleet of Cat-erpillar 785B off-highway trucks equipped with Caterpillar’s VitalInformation Management System(VIMS)and Truck PayloadMonitoring System(TPMS)data recording systems. TheVIMS/TPMSsystem continuously recorded machine production andpayload data. The research fleet accumulated more than 14,000operating hours, and hauled close to 8 million tons of materialduring the study period.Delineating the 75th PercentileThe data analysis sought to relate, through a ratio, the 75th per-centile and the mode of each truck haul activity’s duration. Sorting the data by loadrange facilitated the analysis of the variation of ratio with respectto the vehicle gross weight.To quantify the ratio for the hauling operation, the data weresorted into the same load groupings, and additionally into 16 dis-tance ranges. The distance range was from 0.1 mil to 3.3 mil di-vided in increments of 0.2 mil. The ratio of the mode and the 75thpercentile duration for the hauling activity is expressed as a func-tion of the distance traveled. For the ratio of the mode and the75th percentile for the return operation, the data was sorted into16 distance ranges. The ratio varies as the travel distance in-creases.It is believed that because of the large number of sample ob-servations, the ratios obtained are statistically significant and rep-resentative of similar operations.
